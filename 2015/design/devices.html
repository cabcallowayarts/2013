CELLPHONES

For every 1 million cell phones that are recycled, 35,274 lbs of copper, 772 lbs of silver, 75 lbs of gold, and 33 lbs of palladium can be recovered.

When not properly disposed, cellphones can add lead, arsenic, and chlorine into the environment. These compounds are linked to damage of the reproductive, blood, and nervous system. In high doses, arsenic poisoning is lethal. These compounds also lead to tissue damage and the destruction of cell structure.

STEP 1

Clear the device of all personal information.

STEP 2

Research cell phone companies that have programs to refurbish mobile devices or recycle their components.

STEP 3

Donate your device for charitable purposes

Given the sharp rise in the popularity of personal computers of the past forty years, the amount of computers being improperly disposed of has sharply risen. Recycling 1 million laptops saves the energy equivalent to the electricity used by 3,657 U.S. homes in a year.

STEP 1

Backup all of your storage on an external HDD or SSD

STEP 2

Clear your laptop of all your personal information

STEP 3

Locate a computer recycling or trade-in center

BATTERIES

Batteries are considered hazardous because of the metals and/or other toxic or corrosive materials contained within. Batteries are potentially a valuable source of recyclable metal.

STEP 1

Gather batteries in a separate container from the rest of your trash

STEP 2

Find a local recycling center for your batteries

STEP 3

Take the batteries to the recycling center

TELEVISIONS

Although e-waste accounts for only 1 to 4 percent of municipal waste, it may be responsible for as much as 70 percent of the heavy metals in landfills, including 40 percent of all lead. Many of these metals come from televisions. products.

STEP 1

Locate an e-waste recycling center that can take televisions

STEP 2

Safely secure your TV for delivery to the recycling center. Sometimes pick-up is available
